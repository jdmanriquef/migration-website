RUSSIA

How is migration in Russia

Migration to and from Russia is a complex issue with various factors influencing it. Russia is both a destination for many immigrants and a source of emigration for some of its citizens. The net migration rate is a net inflow of 1.7 migrants per 1,000 population, according to 2023 estimates.

Immigration

Russia attracts immigrants, particularly from the Commonwealth of Independent States (CIS) countries like Tajikistan, Kyrgyzstan, Ukraine, Armenia, Kazakhstan, and Uzbekistan. The government also encourages immigration of ethnic Russians.

Emigration

A significant number of Russians emigrate, with many choosing CIS countries or those near the CIS region as their destinations. The desire for better opportunities, a better quality of life, and concerns about the economic situation in Russia are common reasons for emigration.

Post soviet labor migration

Millions of immigrants from former Soviet republics fill key sectors in Russia's economy, helping to offset demographic challenges and supporting their home communities.

Legal vs illegal migration

Both legal and illegal immigration are prevalent in Russia. Legal immigrants may receive grant money or low-interest loans from Rossotrudnichestvo (the International russian aid agency).

Impact on the economy

Migrant workers have become an indispensable part of Russia's urban life, forming a significant portion of the workforce, particularly in low-skilled jobs.

Challenges and concerns

Some migrants face challenges, such as xenophobia and discrimination, and the Russian government has been criticized for its migration policies.

How easy is to migrate to Russia

Russia's immigration policy is one of the world's most liberal policies. This country gives its citizenship to the individuals who live in Russia for five years and pass the Russian language exam under the current law.

what is the cost of living in Russia

The cost of living in Russia is generally lower than in many Western countries, but expenses can vary significantly between major cities like Moscow and Saint Petersburg and smaller regional towns. For a single person, monthly living costs can range from 20,000 to 35,000 rubles (250 to 450 dollars), while a family of four might spend between 120,000 and 150,000 rubles per month
